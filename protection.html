The protection branch of the Secret Service wasn’t made official for many years after Secret Service began as a department. After three assassinations, it was decided the President needed protection. It took congress a little bit to get funds to fund the protection of the President (Reese, 2013). Protection of the people around the President grew over the years. Protections now included “executive mansion and grounds, building with Presidential offices, President and immediate family, foreign diplomatic missions located in the metropolitan DC area, foreign diplomatic missions located in the United States, its territories, and its possessions” (Reese, 2013). The Protection detail has swallowed up Executive Protective Service (protected the white house grounds) and Treasury Police Force. This fusion is referred to as the Secret Service Uniformed Division. Protection even expanded more to protect certain property, federal officials including their families, and Vice President’s spouse (Reese, 2013). The agency was even given protection and planning responsibilities of national events, the National Special Security Events (NSSEs). National Threat Assessment Center (NTAC) was also made to train agencies for “threat assessment training, consulting on complex threat assessment cases, researching threat assessment and targeted violence, promoting standardization of federal, state, and local threat assessments and investigations and other threat assessment activities as determined by the Department of Homeland Security (DHS)” (Reese, 2013). Protection division of the Secret Service has more revisions and has had more add-ons than the investigation division. It also receives 63% of funding and also has three times the man power (Reese, 2013). Thanks to the protection being so large and more in the media with protecting important figures, they are the more remembered division.
